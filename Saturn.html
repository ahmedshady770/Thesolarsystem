the Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant with an average radius about nine times that of Earth. It has only one-eighth the average density of Earth, but with its larger volume Saturn is over 95 times more massive. Saturn is named after the Roman god of agriculture; its astronomical symbol (♄) represents the god's sickle. Saturn's interior is probably composed of a core of iron–nickel and rock (silicon and oxygen compounds). This core is surrounded by a deep layer of metallic hydrogen, an intermediate layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and finally outside the Frenkel line a gaseous outer layer. Saturn has a pale yellow hue due to ammonia crystals in its upper atmosphere. Electrical current within the metallic hydrogen layer is thought to give rise to Saturn's planetary magnetic field, which is weaker than Earth's, but has a magnetic moment 580 times that of Earth due to Saturn's larger size. Saturn's rotation causes it to have the shape of an oblate spheroid; that is, it is flattened at the poles and bulges at its equator. Its equatorial and polar radii differ by almost 10%: 60,268 km versus 54,364 km respectively. Jupiter, Uranus, and Neptune also show this difference in shape. Saturn is the only planet of the Solar System that is less dense than water—about 30% less—and so would float if placed in water. Saturn's rings are made up mostly of ice particles with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust. They range in size from tens of meters to several kilometers wide and are composed mostly of water ice with some contamination from dust particles incorporated into their structure as well as organic compounds. The rings are extremely thin: most are about 10 meters thick while some are just one meter thick or less. In total they span about 250,000 km in diameter but their total mass amounts to just over 3×10^19 kg—less than 0.0001% that of Saturn itself! The ring system consists mainly of seven main rings: A through G (with gaps between them). The rings are named according to their relative location around Saturn: A being closest to the planet followed by B then C etc., outwards towards G which lies farthest away from Saturn itself. The Cassini–Huygens mission was launched on October 15th 1997 with an aim to study both Saturn and its moons as well as its ring system in detail using advanced instruments such as cameras, spectrometers etc.. The mission was successful in achieving all its goals including discovering new moons around Saturn such as Enceladus which was found to have active geysers spewing out water vapor from beneath its icy surface! Saturn has 62 known moons (as per July 2020) ranging from tiny moonlets just few kilometers across up to Titan which has a diameter greater than that of Mercury! Titan is also unique among all other moons because it has an atmosphere composed mainly nitrogen along with traces other gases such as methane and ethane making it one most interesting objects for study within our Solar System! In addition to studying Titan’s atmosphere scientists have also been able map out features on Titan’s surface such as lakes filled with liquid hydrocarbons like methane or ethane! Cup and Handle: This is a chart pattern that forms when the price of a digital currency makes an upward move, followed by a period of consolidation or sideways movement, and then another upward move. The first upward move forms the cup, while the consolidation period forms the handle. This pattern usually signals that the price of the digital currency is about to make another upward move. 3. Double Top/Bottom: This is a chart pattern that forms when the price of a digital currency makes two consecutive peaks or troughs at roughly the same level. The double top signals that the price of the digital currency is about to reverse direction and start falling, while a double bottom signals that it is about to start rising again. 4. Triangle Patterns: There are three types of triangle patterns – ascending triangles, descending triangles, and symmetrical triangles – which all signal that there may be an upcoming breakout in either direction depending on which type it is. An ascending triangle signals an upcoming bullish breakout, while a descending triangle signals an upcoming bearish breakout. A symmetrical triangle can signal either direction depending on which way it breaks out from its apex point.
