The Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the second-smallest planet in the Solar System, after Mercury. It is named after the Roman god of war, Mars. It is a terrestrial planet with a thin atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide. The surface of Mars is covered with dust and rocks, and its surface features include polar ice caps, volcanoes, valleys, deserts and canyons. The rotational period and seasonal cycles of Mars are similar to those of Earth, as is the tilt that produces the seasons. Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are small and irregularly shaped. These may be captured asteroids, similar to 5261 Eureka, a Martian trojan asteroid. Mars has an average distance from the Sun of about 227 million km (141 million mi) and an orbital period of 687 (Earth) days. The axial tilt of Mars is 25° compared to Earth's 23°27'. Its day length is 24 hours 37 minutes 22 seconds compared to Earth's 24 hours. Its gravity is 3.711 m/s2 compared to Earth's 9.807 m/s2. The atmosphere on Mars consists mostly of carbon dioxide (95%), nitrogen (2%) and argon (1%). There are trace amounts of oxygen (0.13%) and water vapor (0.03%). The atmospheric pressure on Mars ranges from 6–10 mbar at the surface level up to 0.6 mbar at its highest point in the atmosphere—about twice as high as on Earth's Moon but still less than 1% that on Earth at sea level. Mars is the site of Olympus Mons, the largest volcano and second-highest known mountain in the Solar System, and of Valles Marineris, one of the largest canyons in the Solar System. The smooth Borealis basin in the northern hemisphere covers 40% of the planet and may be a giant impact feature. Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are small and irregularly shaped. These may be captured asteroids, similar to 5261 Eureka, a Martian trojan asteroid. There are ongoing investigations assessing whether these bodies could be suitable hosts for human colonization. The surface gravity on Mars is only about 38% that of Earth's; combined with its lower atmospheric pressure (about 1% that of Earth's), this means that astronauts would need to use pressurized suits when exploring its surface. Its atmospheric pressure has also been found to vary significantly over short periods (minutes) due to strong dust storms. The temperature on Mars can range from −87 °C (−125 °F) at its coldest during winter in its polar regions to up to 35 °C (95 °F) during summer at its equator; however temperatures rarely exceed 20 °C (68 °F). Mars has an extremely thin atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide (95%), nitrogen (2.7%) and argon (1.6%). It also contains trace levels of water vapor, oxygen, carbon monoxide and other gases. The atmosphere on Mars is much too thin for liquid water to exist on its surface for any length of time; however there is evidence that liquid brines may exist near or beneath its surface in certain areas where temperatures remain above freezing point year round due to geothermal heating from below or from seasonal melting caused by higher levels of insolation during summer months at higher latitudes near its poles. The climate on Mars varies greatly depending on latitude; while temperatures near its equator can reach up to 35°C during summer months they can drop down as low as -87°C during winter months at higher latitudes near its poles where most ice deposits are located; however average temperatures remain below freezing point year round due to lack of an ozone layer which would otherwise trap heat from sunlight more effectively like it does on Earth’s atmosphere. The Martian day or sol lasts 24 hours 39 minutes 35 seconds which is slightly longer than an Earth day due to slight differences in their respective orbital periods around their parent star; this difference causes days on Mars to appear slightly longer than those on Earth when observed from space or through telescopes from Earth’s surface although they are still considered equal length days since both planets rotate around their axis once every 24 hours relative to their own reference frames respectively .
